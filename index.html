This is what Mark looks like.

Mark E. Whiting is a PhD candidate advised by Jon Cagan and Phil Leduc at CMU. His work focuses on making creative decisions computationally.

Mark E. Whiting is working to enable computers to design using computational representations of intuition. Though nascent, this field stands to be an important aspect of the way we make creative decisions soon.

Mark is a PhD candidate advised by Jon Cagan and Phil Leduc in Mech Eng at CMU. His thesis work is on the use of automated grammar induction (DCC 2016) for identifying structural patterns in design and biological domains, which is a critical step in more widespread automation of creative decision making.

Mark is intrigued by the future of work and the roll it will have in improving societal productivity and creativity. The Stanford Crowd Research Initiative is an active, large scale collaboration that Mark works with. Together they have improved worker reputation signals (CSCW 2017) and introduced incentive compatibility for workers and requesters (UIST 2016). Daemo, their open source crowdsourcing platform (UIST 2015), is now live, and showcases a new approach to online open platform governance they developed.

Mark mentors students at CMU and in the iSchool Inclusion Institute of Information Science (i3) at the University of Pittsburgh. They have studied how product reviews can be used to innovate at scale (iConference 2016), how research on microtask crowdsourcing can be facilitated through abstraction (forthcoming), and how novice crowd workers can conduct advanced engineering design tasks.

Mark has also collaborated with Yosef Shuman to develop a definitive (and popular) answer to the question: What is service design? and with Windfire Designs to develop the Windfire Circle Tool, the best way for makers to cut circles.

Mark previously studied Industrial Design (MS, KAIST in Korea; BDes, RMIT in Australia; exchange, ZJU in China). He was honored with Red Dot awards in 2006 and 2009 for his design work.

Though this work may seem to explore many unrelated topics, there is a central notion that is of utmost importance. The way we as a society and as individuals make creative decisions is one of our most human aspects. Through understanding grammar rule induction from design, learning to make individual agents in a crowd perform sophisticated judgments and creative tasks, and developing ways to better communicate about subtle design topics, Mark is developing a method for automating design.
